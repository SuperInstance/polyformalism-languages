# Problem Library for Polyformalism Experiments

Each problem includes an English statement and a note on what makes it interesting for cross-linguistic analysis — i.e., which grammatical/cognitive differences are likely to produce divergent insights.

---

## Algorithmic Problems

### A1: Sorting a Library

**English Statement:** You have 1,000 books with no catalog. Design a method to organize them so anyone can find any book within 2 minutes.

**Cross-linguistic interest:** "Organize" and "find" map to fundamentally different operations across languages. Finnish's 15 cases might produce a spatially-rich categorization scheme. Navajo's shape/classificatory verbs might sort by physical properties English wouldn't consider. Classical Chinese's topic-prominence might produce a multi-axis system rather than a hierarchical one. Arabic's root-and-pattern morphology might suggest organizing by "deep structure" relationships between subjects.

---

### A2: Search in an Unknown Space

**English Statement:** You're in a dark room you've never been in. You need to find the door. You can feel the walls but can't see. Design a strategy that guarantees you find the door.

**Cross-linguistic interest:** Spatial reasoning varies dramatically across languages. Kuuk Thaayorre speakers use cardinal directions — would they impose an absolute frame on an unknown space? Finnish speakers might leverage case-based spatial distinctions (inessive "in", adessive "on", abessive "without"). Russian's directional prefixes (в-, вы-, при-, у-) might decompose the search into directional primitives. Navajo's motion verbs with shape-classifiers might categorize the tactile feedback differently.

---

### A3: Optimization — The Traveling Philosopher

**English Statement:** A philosopher must visit 7 cities to give lectures, starting and ending at home. The roads between cities have different costs (time, money, danger). Find the optimal route.

**Cross-linguistic interest:** "Optimal" is culturally loaded. English defaults to minimizing one metric (usually time or money). Japanese's honorific system might introduce social obligation costs. Quechua's evidentiality might require different certainty levels for road safety claims. Russian's perfective/imperfective distinction might frame the journey as either a completed whole or an ongoing process, changing what's being optimized. Classical Chinese might optimize for relational harmony between visits rather than distance.

---

### A4: Scheduling — The Feast

**English Statement:** 20 families want to share a communal kitchen. Each family needs 2 hours, the kitchen is open 12 hours/day, and no two families who are feuding can use the kitchen on the same day. Design a fair schedule.

**Cross-linguistic interest:** "Fair" is deeply language-dependent. Japanese social grammar makes the feud-constraint more nuanced (degrees of conflict). Yoruba's noun classification might group families differently. Nahuatl's noun-incorporation might merge "family" and "kitchen-use" into a single concept that changes the scheduling primitive. Finnish's partitive case might naturally produce a "partial usage" framing that English lacks.

---

### A5: Resource Allocation — The Garden

**English Statement:** You have a plot of land (10m × 10m) and seeds for 5 crops. Each crop needs different amounts of sun, water, and soil depth. Some help each other (companion planting), some harm each other. Design the planting layout.

**Cross-linguistic interest:** Spatial arrangement and relational thinking. Classical Chinese's topic-prominence and serial verbs might describe the layout as a sequence of actions rather than a static grid. Navajo's classificatory verbs handle objects by shape — seeds, growing plants, and harvested crops might be different "shapes," producing a temporal layout. Arabic's root-and-pattern system might see the crops as variations on a pattern (cultivation-root + sun/water/soil patterns).

## Systems Problems

### S1: Caching — The Village Memory

**English Statement:** A village has one elder who remembers everything but takes a day to answer any question. Design a system where villagers can get answers to common questions without waiting for the elder.

**Cross-linguistic interest:** "Memory" and "knowledge" are grammatically distinct across languages. Quechua's evidentiality system would immediately categorize cached answers as hearsay (-si) vs. elder's direct knowledge (-mi), producing a natural cache-freshness system. Russian's aspect might distinguish between "the answer that was given" (perfective) and "the answer that remains valid" (imperfective). Classical Chinese's lack of tense might collapse the temporal dimension in interesting ways.

---

### S2: Distribution — The River Network

**English Statement:** Water must be distributed from a mountain lake to 50 villages through a network of canals. Canals can break. Design a system that ensures every village gets water even if any 3 canals break simultaneously.

**Cross-linguistic interest:** Network reliability through different cognitive lenses. Finnish's case system might model canal-breakage as a case transformation (adessive → abessive: "with canal" → "without canal"). Japanese's topic-subject distinction might separate "as for water" (topic) from "the canal breaks" (subject), creating a redundant reasoning layer. Navajo's event-based grammar might frame canal-breakage as an event-shape rather than a state-change.

---

### S3: Fault Tolerance — The Night Watch

**English Statement:** 5 guards patrol a wall at night. Each guard can only stay awake 6 hours. Any guard might fall asleep without warning. Design a watch system that guarantees the wall is always monitored.

**Cross-linguistic interest:** Reliability and trust through different grammatical lenses. Korean's honorific system introduces social hierarchy into the watch design — who watches whom? Quechua's evidentiality forces explicit tracking of how we *know* each guard is awake. Arabic's dual number might naturally pair guards, producing a buddy-system that English wouldn't default to.

---

### S4: Monitoring — The Forest Watcher

**English Statement:** You must monitor a vast forest for fires. You can place sensors that detect smoke, but each sensor costs money and has limited range. How many sensors do you need, where do you place them, and how do you know the system is working?

**Cross-linguistic interest:** Epistemic monitoring. Quechua's evidential system naturally produces "how do we know what we know?" — meta-monitoring built into the grammar. Yoruba's tonal system might metaphorically map to signal differentiation. Finnish's partitive case might model "partial coverage" as a first-class concept rather than a deficiency.

---

### S5: Scaling — The Growing Town

**English Statement:** A town of 100 people has a well. The town will grow to 10,000 people over 10 years. The well can't serve more than 500. Design a water system that scales with the population.

**Cross-linguistic interest:** Growth and change across languages. Russian's perfective/imperfective distinction might separate the *act* of building new infrastructure from the *state* of having it. Navajo's verb-centric grammar might frame the entire problem as a motion from small to large. Classical Chinese's topic-prominence might keep "the town" as a stable topic while everything else changes around it — a different architectural instinct.

## Creative Problems

### C1: Design a Game

**English Statement:** Design a game that two strangers can play to decide who goes first in a cooperative task. The game must be fair, take less than 1 minute, require no equipment, and both players must feel the outcome was just.

**Cross-linguistic interest:** "Fair" and "just" are deeply language-dependent. Korean's honorific system might produce a game that accounts for social position. Japanese might design a game that preserves both players' dignity (face-saving built into grammar). Nahuatl's noun-incorporation might produce a game where "stranger-meeting" is a single incorporated concept. Arabic's root-and-pattern might produce a game based on linguistic patterns rather than physical actions.

---

### C2: Write a Law

**English Statement:** Write a law that regulates how neighbors must handle a tree whose branches hang over both their properties. The law must be clear, enforceable, and fair to both parties regardless of who planted the tree.

**Cross-linguistic interest:** Legal reasoning through different grammatical lenses. Classical Chinese's topic-prominence might frame the law around "as for the tree" rather than "the tree owner" — fundamentally different legal framing. German's case system might produce a more precisely delineated law. Finnish's partitive might handle "partial ownership" of the tree natively. Russian's aspect pairs might distinguish the ongoing state of branch-overhang from the act of planting.

---

### C3: Create a Ritual

**English Statement:** Create a ritual for a community to perform when a member leaves (moves away). The ritual should help both the departing member and the community process the transition.

**Cross-linguistic interest:** Ritual design is inherently about social cognition. Japanese's honorific system provides built-in tools for marking the social transition. Navajo's event-shape grammar might frame the departure as a type of motion-event with its own shape. Quechua's inclusive/exclusive "we" distinction handles the transition from "we including you" to "we without you" as a grammatical operation. Arabic's root system might derive the ritual name/structure from the same root as "journey" and "separation."

---

### C4: Build a Community

**English Statement:** 30 strangers arrive at an uninhabited island. Design a system for how they make collective decisions in the first month. No prior authority exists. They share one goal: survive.

**Cross-linguistic interest:** Collective decision-making through different cognitive frameworks. Korean's multi-level honorific system provides a natural hierarchy-formation tool. Classical Chinese's topic-prominence might organize decisions around "as for survival" rather than "who decides." Yoruba's serial verbs might chain decision steps into a single conceptual unit. Finnish's lack of grammatical gender might produce a more egalitarian default. Navajo's animacy hierarchy might impose a competence-based (rather than popularity-based) leadership structure.

---

### C5: Invent a Tool

**English Statement:** Invent a tool that helps people remember to do things they keep forgetting. The tool cannot be electronic. It must work for people who can't read.

**Cross-linguistic interest:** Memory and cognition through different linguistic lenses. Quechua's evidentiality might produce a tool that tracks *why* you need to remember (source-tracking). Yoruba's tonal system might produce a musical/auditory reminder tool. Navajo's shape-classifiers might produce a tool organized by the shape of the task. Nahuatl's noun-incorporation might create a "memory-aid" as a single concept-object. Japanese might produce a socially-embedded tool (another person as the reminder system).

## Philosophical Problems

### P1: What Is Fairness?

**English Statement:** Define fairness. Then describe a situation where your definition produces an unintuitive result, and explain whether your definition or your intuition should be trusted.

**Cross-linguistic interest:** This is the core test of linguistic relativity. "Fairness" doesn't exist as a universal concept — each language's cognitive tools will produce a different definition. Finnish (genderless, case-rich, partitive) might define fairness as proportional partiality. Japanese (honorific, contextual) might define it as contextual appropriateness. Navajo (verb-centric, process-oriented) might define it as right-motion/shape-of-events. Classical Chinese (topic-prominent) might define it as relational harmony. The "unintuitive result" test will expose the boundaries of each language's fairness concept.

---

### P2: What Is Beauty?

**English Statement:** Define beauty. Identify something that is beautiful by your definition but that most people would not consider beautiful. Explain why they're wrong.

**Cross-linguistic interest:** Aesthetic cognition varies dramatically. Arabic's root-and-pattern system might define beauty as pattern-completeness. Finnish might define it as instrumental harmony (things fitting their purpose). Classical Chinese might define it as relational resonance between elements. Navajo might define it as right-motion — events unfolding in their proper shape. Yoruba might define it through tonal harmony. The counterintuitive examples will reveal deep cognitive biases.

---

### P3: What Is Knowledge?

**English Statement:** Define knowledge. Then describe something you know that you cannot prove to anyone else. Is it still knowledge?

**Cross-linguistic interest:** Epistemology directly tests evidentiality systems. Quechua has *grammatical knowledge-source tracking* — its definition of knowledge will be qualitatively different from English. Japanese's epistemic sentence-final particles (だろう, かもしれない) encode certainty levels that English handles lexically. Turkish, another evidentiality language, will produce a knowledge taxonomy based on source-type. Finnish's partitive might allow "partial knowledge" as a valid category. This problem most directly tests whether grammar shapes epistemology.

---

### P4: What Is Time?

**English Statement:** Define time. Explain whether time flows, stretches, exists, or is an illusion. Then describe how your concept of time changes when you're waiting for something vs. when you're deeply engaged in an activity.

**Cross-linguistic interest:** Boroditsky's work directly showed Mandarin speakers think vertically about time. Classical Chinese's lack of tense might produce a timeless-present concept. Russian's aspectual system (not tense-dominant) might define time as the contrast between process and completion. Navajo's verb-centric grammar might define time as event-shape succession. Arabic's verb system (past/present/future but no continuous aspect) might produce a more discrete, quantum-like time concept.

---

### P5: What Is Self?

**English Statement:** Define "self." Is the self persistent, constructed, singular, multiple, or an illusion? Describe a situation where your concept of self breaks down.

**Cross-linguistic interest:** "Self" varies enormously across languages. Japanese's subject-drop might suggest the self is context-dependent (only appears when grammatically needed). Classical Chinese's topic-prominence might separate "self as topic" from "self as agent." Korean's inclusive/exclusive distinctions might define self relationally (I vs. we-including-you vs. we-excluding-you). Navajo's polysynthetic incorporation might model self as a bundle of processes rather than an entity. Nahuatl's animacy hierarchy might define self as a position in an animacy scale rather than a discrete thing.
