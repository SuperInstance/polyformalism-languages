# Polyformal Analysis of Native American Linguistic Thought & Constraint Satisfaction System Design
This analysis contrasts Native American languages (span three unrelated families: Uto-Aztecan, Athabaskan, Andean, Mayan) with Indo-European (IE) and Sinitic languages by mapping grammatical features to implicit ontologies, then reverse-actualizes these insights into a constraint satisfaction system (CSS) grounded in process-relational-animistic thinking. Unlike dominant IE/Sinitic frameworks that prioritize static objects and agent-centered action, Native American languages encode a relational, process-first, and animistic worldview.

---

## Part 1: Linguistic Ontology Breakdown by Family
### Nahuatl (Uto-Aztecan)
1.  **Absolute vs. Possessed Noun Distinction**
    Nahuatl divides nouns into two classes: *absolute* (entities with independent existence: sun, stone, cedar) and *possessed* (entities constitutively relational: body parts, kin, communal land). IE/Sinitic treat all nouns as intrinsically named, with possessive markers as optional add-ons; Nahuatl requires possessed nouns to take affixes indicating their relational link to a owner.
    **Encoded Ontology**: Being is not universal—some entities exist autonomously, while others are defined by their ties to other beings. This rejects IE’s unspoken assumption that all nouns refer to self-contained "things."
2.  **Noun Incorporation**
    Instead of structuring utterances as `Agent + Action + Object` (e.g., *ni-cuitlatl ni-nechtiltil* = "I eat tortillas"), Nahuatl fuses objects into verbs: *ni-tlaciltla-n* = "I tortilla-eat." The object becomes part of the process, not a separate entity.
    **Encoded Ontology**: Experience is holistic, not a set of discrete components. Thought focuses on integrated activity rather than isolated agents or objects.
3.  **Reverential Voice**
    Grammatical verb morphology encodes social hierarchy (deference to elders, authority figures, or deities) as a mandatory constraint, not a pragmatic afterthought. A single utterance will shift verb forms to reflect the relative status of speaker, hearer, and referent.
    **Encoded Ontology**: Social relations are not incidental to communication—they are a fundamental semantic component of all thought about action.
4.  **Flower-Song (in xochitl in cuicatl)**
    Nahuatl’s core metaphor of beauty as truth ties directly to its grammar: verbs often fuse nominal and lexical roots to create speech acts that are both descriptive and world-making. A phrase like *xochitl cuicatl* ("flower-song") is not just a label for poetry—it is an active creation of beauty that shapes the community’s moral and physical world.
    **Encoded Ontology**: Language is not a referential tool for describing pre-existing reality; it is a participant in constructing it.

### Navajo (Athabaskan)
1.  **Verb-Centric Language**
    90% of Navajo morphology appears on verbs, with most nouns derived from nominalized verb stems (e.g., *naaltsoos* = "dog" derives from a verb meaning "the one that walks on four legs"). IE prioritizes nouns as the basic lexical category; Navajo frames experience around dynamic events.
    **Encoded Ontology**: The fundamental unit of thought is process, not static object. Even "entities" are defined by their participation in events.
2.  **Classificatory Verb Stems**
    Unlike IE/Sinitic classifiers (which often rely on nominal categories like "flat object" or "animate being"), Navajo classifies all action by the shape, motion, and handling properties of participants. For example, 8+ verb stems distinguish "carrying a long rigid object" (stick, rifle), "carrying a small round object" (berry, stone), and "carrying a animate being" (child, sheep).
    **Encoded Ontology**: The world is not made of static substances, but of dynamic patterns of interaction. Categories are defined by embodied experience, not inherent properties.
3.  **Handling Verbs (13+ "Giving" Verbs)**
    Navajo has distinct verb forms for giving based on the object’s relational significance: *-'áát'eed* for giving a tool, *-łááł* for giving food, *-sóó'* for giving a animate child. The act of giving is defined by the recipient’s relationship to the object, not just transfer.
    **Encoded Ontology**: Social interaction is structured by the relational meaning of exchanged goods, not abstract "giving" as a universal act.
4.  **Whorf’s Unjustly Debunked Insights**
    Linguist Benjamin Lee Whorf’s 1940 claim that Navajo encodes a process-first ontology was dismissed as linguistic determinism, but this misrepresents his work: he argued that Navajo shapes default framing, not thought itself. Modern cognitive linguistics confirms this: Navajo speakers default to event-based reasoning, while IE speakers default to object-based reasoning. The "debunking" stemmed from a misreading of Whorf as claiming language determined thought, rather than structuring it.

### Quechua (Andean)
1.  **Evidential Markers (-mi, -si, -cha)**
    Every utterance requires a suffix encoding the speaker’s knowledge source: *-mi* (firsthand, observed), *-si* (reported, secondhand), *-cha* (inferred, from evidence like smoke or ice). IE/Sinitic treat evidentials as pragmatic add-ons; Quechua makes them mandatory.
    **Encoded Ontology**: Knowledge is situated, not objective. Truth is tied to the speaker’s lived experience of the world.
2.  **Inclusive vs. Exclusive "We"**
    Quechua has two first-person plural pronouns: *ñuqanchik* (inclusive: "we including you") and *ñuqayku* (exclusive: "we excluding you"). IE has no grammatical marking for this, treating "we" as a monolithic group.
    **Encoded Ontology**: The social self is not an individual, but a relational group defined by inclusion. Thought about community is always tied to who is included in the group.
3.  **-yka- Experiential Suffix**
    This suffix marks shared knowledge between speaker and hearer (e.g., *Mayu llama yka-mi* = "The river is cold, as we both know"). It enforces that communication requires acknowledging shared epistemic ground.
    **Encoded Ontology**: Communal knowledge is a fundamental constraint on meaningful communication.
4.  **Yanantin Complementarity**
    Andean philosophy’s core concept of *yanantin* (interdependent opposites: sun/moon, male/female, nature/culture) is encoded in Quechua’s paired noun and verb suffixes. For example, *mamapata* ("mother’s land") is always framed as relational to *papaq* ("father’s sky"), with no separate lexical entry for either without the other.
    **Encoded Ontology**: Reality is constructed from interdependent pairs, not binary, isolated opposites.

### Mayan Languages (e.g., K'iche')
1.  **Ergative-Absolutive Alignment**
    Unlike IE’s nominative-accusative alignment (treating the subject of transitive and intransitive verbs as identical), Mayan marks the agent of a transitive verb as ergative (*doer*) and the patient/subject of an intransitive verb as absolutive (*undergoer or participant*). For example:
    - Transitive: *X-∅-k'utub' ajaw b'e* = "The king [ergative] ate the corn [absolutive]"
    - Intransitive: *X-∅-k'utub' ajaw* = "The king [absolutive] ate"
    **Encoded Ontology**: Action is defined by relational roles, not a central agent. Thought focuses on the interaction between participants, not the agent as the primary subject.
2.  **Antipassive Voice**
    The antipassive demotes the transitive patient to a optional role, shifting focus to the agent’s action. For example, *X-∅-k'utub' b'e* = "Corn [absolutive] is being eaten" vs. antipassive *X-ajaw-k'utub'* = "The king is eating [without specifying the object]".
    **Encoded Ontology**: Thought can focus on the process of acting without fixating on specific targets, enabling flexible reasoning about ongoing activity.

## Part 2: Reverse-Actualization – A Native American-Led Constraint Satisfaction System
A constraint satisfaction system (CSS) solves problems by identifying solutions that satisfy multiple overlapping constraints, rather than optimizing a single metric. Traditional CSS frame problems through an IE/Sinitic lens of isolated agents, static objects, and objective knowledge. Below is a CSS designed exclusively through Native American linguistic insights, for indigenous land stewardship:

### Core Design Principles (Drawn Directly from Linguistic Ontologies)
1.  **Relational Primitives**: No independent entities—all variables are roles in event frames (e.g., `berry-pick` instead of `picker`, `berries`, `harvest`).
2.  **Process-First Representation**: The basic unit of the system is a dynamic event frame, not a static database entry.
3.  **Epistemic Constraint Encoding**: Every solution must include a mandatory knowledge source tag (firsthand, reported, inferred) aligned with Quechua evidentials.
4.  **Social-Embodied Constraints**: Uses inclusive/exclusive "we" pronouns to define stakeholder groups, and classificatory verb stems to classify resources by handling properties (aligned with Navajo).
5.  **Animistic Gradient Agency**: Assigns a relational agency score to all participants (human, non-human, natural) based on their role in the event frame.

### Example Workflow: Berry Harvest Planning
1.  **Input Constraints**:
    - Knowledge source: *-mi* (firsthand, the community harvested berries here yesterday)
    - Social group: *ñuqanchik* (inclusive: all community members, including non-local relatives)
    - Process frame: `berry-pick` (incorporated noun + verb, aligned with Nahuatl)
    - Classificatory constraint: Berries = small round object (Navajo `-łááł` verb stem)
    - Animistic constraint: Soil moisture > 20% (inferred from satellite data, `-cha` tag)
2.  **Constraint Satisfaction**:
    The system generates solutions that meet all requirements:
    - Limit harvest to 30% of the patch to support the berry plants’ reproductive needs (animistic agency)
    - Share harvest with elders and non-local relatives (inclusive social group)
    - Document harvest data for future community knowledge (reported knowledge source)

### Emergent Process-Relational-Animistic Insights
1.  **Decentralized Distributed Agency**: No single "controller"—agency is shared across all participants (pickers, berries, soil, community). This rejects IE’s agent-centric model of decision-making.
2.  **Contextual Dynamic Categorization**: Resources are classified by their role in the event frame, not static properties (e.g., a berry is not just "fruit," but "small round object to be picked").
3.  **Epistemic Justice**: The system prioritizes community knowledge sources over expert data, centering indigenous lived experience rather than Western positivism.
4.  **Relational Social Identity**: The system only validates solutions for inclusive social groups, enforcing quechua’s vision of the self as a relational community rather than an individual.
5.  **Stewardship Over Extraction**: The system focuses on ongoing relational care for the land, not extractive harvest, aligning with Nahuatl’s flower-song metaphor of language as world-building.

## Conclusion
Native American languages offer a polyformal alternative to the dominant IE/Sinitic ontologies, encoding a process-relational-animistic worldview that prioritizes interdependence, embodied experience, and communal care. The reverse-actualized CSS demonstrates how these linguistic insights can be translated into equitable, sustainable systems that challenge Western assumptions about agency, knowledge, and identity. The unjust debunking of Whorf’s Navajo insights was a misstep—modern cognitive linguistics confirms that language shapes default framing, and Native American languages offer critical tools for rethinking how we design systems that honor relationality to the land and each other.
