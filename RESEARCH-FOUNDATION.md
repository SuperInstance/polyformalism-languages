# Linguistic Polyformalism: Research Foundation

## The Hypothesis

**Programming languages constrain computation. Human languages constrain cognition.**

The Sapir-Whorf hypothesis (linguistic relativity) has accumulated substantial empirical support:

### Empirical Evidence for Linguistic Relativity

1. **Boroditsky (2001, 2011)** — Mandarin speakers think about time vertically (上/下), English speakers horizontally (front/back). Learning new spatial metaphors CHANGES temporal reasoning.

2. **Kuuk Thaayorre (Boroditsky)** — Aboriginal Australians use cardinal directions (N/S/E/W) instead of left/right. They maintain absolute orientation at all times — a cognitive ability English speakers lack.

3. **Color perception (MIT 2024)** — Language categories for color influence perception speed and accuracy. Learning a second language ALTERS how the first language divides the spectrum.

4. **Grammatical gender (Boroditsky)** — German speakers describe bridges (feminine "die Brücke") as beautiful/elegant; Spanish speakers describe bridges (masculine "el puente") as strong/dangerous. Same object, different grammar → different properties.

5. **Levinson (2003)** — Spatial language determines spatial memory. Speakers of absolute-frame languages (Guugu Yimithirr, Tzeltal) remember spatial arrangements differently than relative-frame language speakers.

6. **Evidentiality (Quechua, Turkish)** — Speakers of languages with grammatical evidentiality track information SOURCE differently. Quechua speakers must grammatically mark whether they SAW something, HEARD about it, or INFERRED it.

### The Polyformalism Extension

Our framework extends Sapir-Whorf from perception to *creative insight production*:

| Level | What's Constrained | Example |
|-------|-------------------|---------|
| **Perception** | How you see the world | Color categories, spatial orientation |
| **Categorization** | What categories exist | Noun classes (Bantu), grammatical gender |
| **Process** | How change is expressed | Verb aspects, evidentiality |
| **Relationship** | What connections are visible | Topic-prominence, case marking |
| **Insight** | What novel thoughts are possible | **THIS IS OUR RESEARCH QUESTION** |

### The "Constraint" Concept Across Languages

How different languages express "constraint" reveals what each THINKS a constraint IS:

| Language | Word for "Constraint" | Literal Meaning | Implied Metaphysics |
|----------|----------------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| English | constraint | "bind together" | External force limiting action |
| Ancient Greek | πέρας (peras) | "boundary, limit" | The edge of being (Plato's πέρας-ἄπειρον) |
| Greek | ὅρος (horos) | "boundary, definition" | Definition IS constraint |
| Classical Chinese | 約束 (yuēshù) | "bind, restrict" | Social agreement constraining |
| Chinese | 法 (fǎ) | "method, law, pattern" | Pattern AS constraint (not limitation) |
| Navajo | (no direct equivalent) | — | Constraints expressed as SHAPE/MOTION of events |
| Quechua | (no direct equivalent) | — | Constraints expressed with evidential markers (who says so?) |
| Finnish | rajoitus | "limitation, border" | Spatial boundary metaphor |
| Russian | ограничение (ogranichenie) | "boundary-setting" | Setting boundaries (perfective = deliberate act) |
| Arabic | قيد (qayd) | "fetter, shackle" | Physical binding metaphor |
| Japanese | 制約 (seiyaku) | "system-agreement" | Social/systemic constraint |

**The concept of "constraint" varies more than we assume.** English-thinking sees constraints as EXTERNAL FORCES. Greek-thinking sees them as DEFINITIONS (to define is to constrain). Chinese-thinking sees them as PATTERNS. Navajo-thinking has no word for it — constraints are just the SHAPE of events.

### The Experimental Protocol

For each experiment:
1. **Express the problem** in the target language's thinking (not just translation — GRAMMATICAL thinking)
2. **Solve** using only the cognitive tools that language provides
3. **Identify insights** that are natural in this language but impossible/unlikely in English
4. **Cross-reference** with other languages to detect genuinely novel insights
5. **Evaluate** using our neuroscience-informed valuation system (novelty × adequacy)
