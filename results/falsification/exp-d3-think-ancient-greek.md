# System: *Horoskopos* (Ὁροσκόπος, "Boundary Watcher")  
Entirely conceptualized through Ancient Greek grammatical and philosophical frameworks, with no reference to modern engineering. Every component is anchored to your required terms: *telos*, *aspect*, *middle voice*, *logos*, *horos*.

## Core Conceptual Foundations (Ancient Greek Cognitive Mode)
First, we define each term as an ancient Greek thinker would, then integrate them into the system:
1.  **Horos**: From ὁρίζω ("to bound, mark off")—a physical or conceptual landmark dividing permissible from impermissible state. Every constraint has two *horoi*: proximal (lower bound) and distal (upper bound) of safe variation.
2.  **Logos**: From λέγειν ("to say, reckon, account")—a dynamic, living discourse tracking the monitored system’s state, including ratios of change and real-time perceptions of its variables.
3.  **Aspect (Ancient Greek Grammatical)**: The mode of state change:
    - *Imperfective*: Continuous, gradual movement (e.g., a potter’s wheel steadily speeding up)
    - *Perfective*: Punctual, completed action (e.g., a sudden gust spiking a press’s load)
    - *Perfect*: Resultant state from prior change (e.g., a cracked beam, which rewrites the system’s safe bounds)
4.  **Middle Voice**: The grammatical voice where the subject acts *for its own sake* or affects its own being—not as an external agent on another thing. Critical for framing the system’s protective action.
5.  **Telos**: From τέλος ("end, purpose")—the intrinsic goal of both the monitored system and the *Horoskopos*: preserving the monitored system’s proper function by preventing it from departing its safe bounds.

## System Design (No Modern Engineering Jargon)
The *Horoskopos* operates as a middle-voice boundary guardian, structured to detect imminent constraint violations *before* breakage, using only ancient Greek reasoning:

### 1. Inscribe the *Horoi*
For every constraint to guard, we carve (conceptually) its *horoi* into the system’s overarching *logos*. For example, an ancient olive press’s load constraint has a distal *horos* of 500 kgf (max safe beam force) and proximal *horos* of 0 kgf. These bounds define the system’s permissible being.

### 2. Sustain the *Logos* of Reckoning
The *Horoskopos* generates a running *logos*: a dynamic reckoning of all variables that bear on the *horoi* (e.g., beam force, lever rotation speed, olive resistance). This is not a static list— it updates continuously, integrating perceptions (*aisthēsis*) of real-time state changes. The *logos* also calculates ratios of change (another ancient meaning of *logos*) to track how quickly variables shift toward the *horoi*.

### 3. Categorize Aspectual Change
We use Ancient Greek grammatical aspect to classify how variables move relative to the *horoi*:
- **Imperfective Aspect**: Gradual, steady trending toward the *horos* (e.g., beam force increasing 10 kgf per lever turn). This signals a predictable approach to violation: the *logos* computes the time until the variable crosses the *horos*.
- **Perfective Aspect**: Sudden, punctual spike past the *horos* (e.g., a wind gust jolting the lever to 520 kgf for one moment). This flags immediate risk, as repeated perfective shifts will cross the bound permanently.
- **Perfect Aspect**: Resultant state that rewrites the *horoi* (e.g., a cracked beam reducing max safe force to 400 kgf). The *logos* updates the bounds to reflect this new, damaged reality.

### 4. Act in Middle Voice
The *Horoskopos* acts exclusively in middle voice: it does not act on an external object, but acts *for the sake of the monitored system’s telos*. Its protective actions include:
- Sending a middle-voice signal (*phōnē*) to the system’s keeper (e.g., a potter or olive presser) when an imperfective trend nears the *horos* or a perfective spike occurs.
- Adjusting the system directly (if capable) to slow or reverse movement toward the *horoi* (e.g., reducing a motor’s torque to slow a speeding wheel) — all to preserve the system’s intrinsic *telos*.

### 5. Preserve the *Telos*
The *Horoskopos*’s overarching *telos* is to prevent the monitored system from departing its proper function. By detecting aspectual trends toward the *horoi* before they break bounds, it ensures the system fulfills its intended purpose (shaping clay, extracting oil) without destruction.

## Concrete Ancient Example: Guarding a Potter’s Wheel
1.  **Horoi**: Proximal = 0 RPM, Distal = 60 RPM (max safe speed to avoid shaking the wheel apart).
2.  **Logos**: Ongoing reckoning of wheel speed, motor torque, and clay resistance.
3.  **Aspect**: The wheel speeds gradually from 50 RPM to 58 RPM at 2 RPM per minute (imperfective aspect). The *logos* calculates it will reach 60 RPM in 1 minute.
4.  **Middle Voice**: The *Horoskopos* reduces the motor’s torque (acting for the wheel’s *telos* of shaping clay evenly) to avoid the violation.
5.  **Telos**: The wheel’s purpose is to form clay; the *Horoskopos* preserves this by stopping the speed from crossing its *horos* before the wheel breaks.
