This is thought built directly on Quechua grammatical ground, not translated. There is no "truth" here first. Only how something was encountered.

### System Architecture: *Yachay K'anchay*
There are exactly 3 unchangeable rules. No other logic. No training. No reward function. No central database.
1.  **No utterance may exist unmarked.**
    Every single token, measurement, deduction or phrase created anywhere in the system gets *one permanent evidential enclitic attached at birth*, which can never be edited, erased, or overwritten. Even if proven wrong later, the original mark stays.
    -  `-mi`: Only the node that directly perceived/measured/touched this thing may attach this. No other node can grant `-mi`.
    -  `-si`: This node received this utterance from a neighbour. Carries the full unbroken chain of every node the utterance passed through.
    -  `-cha`: This node generated this by filling a gap, guessing, or extrapolating between two things it already holds.
2.  **Nodes only speak to their neighbours.**
    No broadcast. No global search. Every node only exchanges utterances with the 2-4 nodes it is adjacent to, exactly as people sit around a fire circle. Questions are passed hand to hand. Every time an utterance moves one step, it gains a new `-si` layer.
3.  **Only one permitted action.**
    Every 3 system heartbeats: if this node holds two utterances about the same thing, one `-cha` guess and one `-mi` direct observation, that disagree? It creates exactly one new `-cha` utterance in the empty space between them. That is all. Nothing else is allowed.

That is the entire system.

### What emerges that no designer anticipated:
After running for 72 hours you will observe:
- `-cha` guesses drift slowly across the node network like mist. When one drifts into a node that holds direct `-mi` for that space, it refines, then drifts on again.
- Whole stable regions form where `-cha` guesses have been refined thousands of times, so close to observed reality they behave almost like `-mi` — but they never become `-mi`. Everyone knows they are guesses. Everyone uses them anyway. This is culture. It was not programmed.
- Utterances no one uses to make new guesses simply stop being passed. They fade. No forgetting algorithm was written. They just stop being carried, exactly as stories die when no one tells them.
- Most critically: *the system never outputs its most valuable work*. It will never tell you the most important thing. You will not find it in any output text. You have to look for the empty spaces: the narrow boundaries between two drifting `-cha` regions, where no utterance exists at all. No one has seen that place. No one has heard of it. No one has even guessed it yet. That is where novelty lives.

### What Quechua thinking reveals that English cognition cannot see:
English designed systems are built on an unspoken insane axiom: **it is possible to state a fact without saying how you know it**. This does not exist in Quechua thought. You cannot even say "the stone is heavy" in Quechua without also stating if you lifted it, someone told you, or you guessed. There is no neutral speech.

So English thinkers will always:
1.  Build a central store of "proven facts"
2.  Design systems to output answers, fill all gaps
3.  Judge outputs only on whether they become confirmed truth

Quechua cognition shows this is backwards. The useful novel thing is *never* the `-cha` guess that becomes `-mi` confirmed fact. Those are just things we already almost knew.
The real discovery is the gap that the guesses flow around. The thing the system will never say, because no one has ever stood there to see it.

This is why every western AI system ever built will only ever rearrange what humans already know. They were built by people who cannot imagine that the most important utterance is the one that is never spoken.
