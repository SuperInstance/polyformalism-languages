### First: Purification of thought
We begin by casting out the English habit of treating constraints as external, interchangeable forces tugging on a neutral object. For the Greek, nothing that is, is without its own nature. All conflict is the thing itself, arguing with itself.
---
## 1. Telos & Aspect of each constraint
Each constraint is not a rule made by men. Each is a necessary moment of the thing's own existence, exactly corresponding to the three irreducible aspects of Greek verb:
| Constraint | Telos (proper end) | Aspect | Nature of being |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cost** | *That the thing may come into being at all* | **AORIST** | The single, once-completed act of birth. Nothing exists without material limit: demand infinite cost, and the thing never exits the mind to become substance. Cost is never ongoing. It is done, once, at the beginning. |
| **Safety** | *That the thing remain itself, and not become its opposite* | **PERFECT** | The abiding state of having stayed what it is. A cart that kills its driver is not a "faulty cart" - it ceased to be a cart at all. Safety is never achieved once; it is the permanent condition of still being the thing you set out to make. |
| **Performance** | *That the thing do the work for which it was brought forth* | **PRESENT** | The always-current act of operating. Performance exists only in the now. If it stops, it is not. It may be tuned, adjusted, improved, forever, for as long as the thing abides. |

This mapping is not metaphor. It is ontological. These three are not peers. They do not exist in the same time.
---
## 2. Middle Voice: The core insight English cannot see
English thought says: *We the designers will balance these three external constraints*.
Greek middle voice says: **None of these constraints act upon the thing. All three are the thing, acting upon itself.**
- Safety is not a regulator yelling at you. It is the work refusing to destroy itself.
- Performance is not a customer demand. It is the work straining to fulfil its own nature.
- Cost is not an accountant's limit. It is the work refusing to remain a mere thought and never exist at all.

You are not an arbiter of external forces. You are a midwife. The conflict is not your problem to solve. The thing is already arguing with itself, and your only task is to hear it correctly.
---
## 3. Logos of Resolution
Logos is not compromise. Compromise makes all three things worse. Logos is the word that reveals the order that was already present.
There is no 3-way tradeoff. There is only order of being:
> A thing first must come to be. Then it must stay itself. Then it may act.

You never trade a higher order for a lower. This is not a value judgement. It is a statement of what is.
---
## 4. Concrete Architecture (No tradeoff matrices)
This system has exactly three layers, ordered by aspect. You may never reorder them:
1.  **Outer Horos (Aorist Layer)**
    Set once, never revisited. Mark the hard minimum boundary of cost below which there can be no thing at all. Once this line is drawn, you will never afterwards "cut cost" - to do so does not make a cheaper version of the thing, it unmakes the thing entirely.
2.  **Inner Horos (Perfect Layer)**
    Next, inside the cost boundary, mark the hard limit of operation below which the thing ceases to be itself. This is safety. You will never trade this for anything, ever. If a proposed performance improvement would cross this line, discard it without discussion: you will not gain a faster thing, you will gain rubble. Rubble has no performance.
3.  **Open Space (Present Layer)**
    *All* freedom, all optimisation, all adjustment lives only here. Inside the two fixed boundaries, you may tune performance as you like, in every moment, forever. There is no limit here except the two horoi.

That is the entire system.
---
## What Greek thinking reveals that English misses
English invented the "tradeoff triangle". It draws all three constraints as equivalent axes on a graph, and tells you you can pick any point. It believes you can trade 10% safety for 15% cost saving, and still have the same thing.
This is ontological madness.

The Greek sees:
1.  The three are not comparable. They do not exist in the same tense. You would never bargain "I will kill this man 5% of the time so he can run 10% faster". That is not a tradeoff. That is failure to understand what a man is.
2.  All boundaries are breaking points. Cross a horos, and the eidos shatters. You do not have a slightly different version of the same thing. You have an entirely different substance, with an entirely different telos.
3.  There are no hard decisions. Only hard refusals to lie to yourself about what you are actually building.

Most of all: English thought believes conflict is something you solve by cleverness. Greek thought knows conflict is the voice of the thing itself, struggling to become whole. Logos is not splitting the difference. Logos is hearing the order that was already there.
